Show It Once, Automate It Forever: OpenAI's New Record & Replay Turns Your Daily Tasks Into AI Skills

June 23, 2026
📖 9 min read
✍️ Sayfe.ai
News & Trends
9 min read

For three years, the unspoken catch with AI automation was this: to get a computer to do your job, you first had to learn how to describe your job to a computer. That meant writing detailed prompts, stitching together no-code tools, or paying someone who could. The work of automating the work often cost more time than the work itself.

On June 18, 2026, OpenAI quietly removed that catch. A new feature called Record & Replay, shipped in version 26.616 of the Codex app for macOS, lets you do something deceptively simple: perform a task on your computer once, while ChatGPT watches, and it turns what you did into a reusable AI skill you can run again anytime — with no script, no prompt engineering, and no code.

The tagline from the launch coverage said it best: "Show it once, skip the script." For small businesses drowning in repetitive admin work, this is one of the most quietly important things OpenAI has shipped all year. Here's what it does, what it can't do yet, and how to put it to work.

The one-sentence version: Record & Replay watches you complete a task — filing an expense, downloading a weekly report, configuring a ticket — and writes the steps into an editable skill file that ChatGPT can re-run later, swapping in new dates, files, or inputs each time.

What Record & Replay Actually Does

Most automation tools ask you to tell the machine what to do. Record & Replay asks you to show it. The difference is the whole story.

When you start a recording, Codex observes your screen as you click through a real task: which windows you open, which buttons you press, what you type, and in what order. When you finish, the AI analyzes that sequence and converts it into a reusable, editable skill. Under the hood, those steps are saved as a plain-text SKILL.md file — a human-readable recipe of the workflow that you can open, read, and edit like a document.

The clever part is that the skill is not a brittle, pixel-for-pixel macro. It accepts variable inputs. Record yourself downloading "May's sales report" today, and next month you can run the same skill for June — the AI understands that the date is a variable, not a fixed value. The recorded skill can then be executed through Codex, Computer Use, browser actions, plugins, or a combination of all of them.

An analogy: a recipe, not a player piano

Older screen-recording macros were like a player piano roll — they replayed the exact keystrokes you punched, and the moment anything moved on screen, the whole thing fell apart. Record & Replay is more like writing down a recipe. A recipe says "add two eggs," not "move your hand 14 inches to the left and grasp." Because the skill captures intent and structure — not just coordinates — it can adapt when the layout shifts or the input changes. That adaptability is exactly what made earlier macro tools useless for real business work, and it's why this release matters.

The headline isn't that Record & Replay is more powerful than every automation tool on the market — it isn't, and we'll get to the limits below. The headline is that it collapses the cost of getting started to nearly zero. The biggest reason small businesses never automate isn't that the tools don't exist; it's that nobody on the team has the time or the skill to build the automation. Record & Replay removes that barrier entirely.

5 Tasks Small Business Teams Should Record First

OpenAI's own launch examples — filing an expense, booking a parking space, creating a correctly configured issue, publishing a video, downloading a recurring report — are a hint at the sweet spot: repetitive, multi-step, low-judgment tasks that follow the same path every time. Here are five worth recording in your first week:

1. The Recurring Report Pull

Every Monday someone logs into your analytics, ad, or POS dashboard, sets the date range, exports a CSV, and drops it in a folder. Record it once. Re-run it weekly with the date as a variable.

2. Expense & Invoice Filing

Uploading receipts, tagging categories, and submitting in your accounting tool is pure repetition. Record the path once and replay it for each new receipt batch.

3. New-Client Onboarding Setup

Creating the folder structure, the project board, and the welcome doc for every new client follows the same checklist. Turn that checklist into a skill new hires can run on day one.

4. Configured Ticket / Issue Creation

Filing a bug or support ticket with the right labels, priority, and assignee — done correctly every time — is exactly the "correctly configured issue" example OpenAI highlighted.

5. Content Publishing Checklist

Uploading a video, setting the thumbnail, tags, and description across platforms is a fixed sequence. Record once, then replay for every new piece of content.

Notice the pattern: none of these require judgment. They require consistency — doing the same eight steps the same way, reliably, without forgetting one. That's precisely the kind of work humans are worst at and machines are best at. How to Record Your First Skill

If you're on a supported plan and using a Mac, getting started takes minutes:

  1. Update the Codex app. Record & Replay shipped in Codex app version 26.616 for macOS. Make sure you're on that build or later.
  2. Enable Computer Use. The feature requires Computer Use to be turned on, since the skill needs permission to observe and later act on your screen.
  3. Pick a clean, repeatable task. Start with something low-stakes and consistent — a report download or a folder setup — not a high-risk action like sending money.
  4. Record once, deliberately. Move through the task at a normal pace. Avoid detours and dead-ends; a clean recording produces a cleaner skill.
  5. Review the SKILL.md file. Open the generated skill and read it. Confirm the steps are right and mark which values (dates, filenames, client names) should be variables.
  6. Replay with a test input. Run the skill once with safe test data and watch it execute before you trust it with the real thing.
  7. Refine and reuse. Edit the SKILL.md to fix any rough edges, then run it whenever the task comes up.
⚠️ Start with read-and-retrieve tasks, not money-movement tasks. Because a recorded skill can take real actions on your screen, the safest first projects are ones that gather or organize information (downloading reports, filing documents) rather than ones that spend money, send external messages, or change production data. Build trust with low-stakes skills first, then expand.

The Honest Limitations (Read This Before You Get Excited)

We're an OpenAI partner, and we'd rather you go in clear-eyed than disappointed. Record & Replay is genuinely useful, but it's early, and it has real constraints today:

  • macOS only. At launch, the feature runs only on Mac. Windows and Linux users are out for now, which matters because most small business fleets are Windows-heavy.
  • Geographic limits. At launch it is unavailable in the European Economic Area, the United Kingdom, and Switzerland — likely tied to regulatory review around Computer Use.
  • Requires Computer Use. Granting an AI permission to watch and act on your screen is a meaningful trust decision. It belongs only on managed devices with a clear understanding of what the skill can touch.
  • Best for stable, repetitive flows. OpenAI is explicit that this shines on tasks that are "easier to show than describe" and that stay consistent. Workflows that change constantly or require human judgment at each step are a poor fit.
  • Plan-gated. It's available to ChatGPT Plus, Pro, Business, Enterprise, and Edu subscribers — not the free tier — and credit-based usage applies under your plan's terms.

None of these are dealbreakers; they're the normal shape of a v1 feature. But if your team runs Windows or sits in the EU, this is one to watch rather than adopt today.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is OpenAI's Record & Replay feature? ▼

Record & Replay is a Codex app feature for macOS, launched June 18, 2026 in version 26.616. It lets you demonstrate a task on your computer once while the AI watches, then turns those steps into a reusable, editable skill (saved as a SKILL.md file) that ChatGPT can run again later. The skill accepts variable inputs like new dates or files, so the same recording works for recurring tasks. It's available to ChatGPT Plus, Pro, Business, Enterprise, and Edu subscribers.

Do I need to write code or prompts to use Record & Replay? ▼

No. That's the entire point of the feature. Instead of writing prompts, scripts, or workflow rules, you simply perform the task once and the AI builds the skill for you. You can optionally open and edit the generated SKILL.md file in plain text to refine the steps or mark which values should be variables, but no programming is required to create or run a skill.

Is Record & Replay available on Windows? ▼

Not at launch. As of June 2026, Record & Replay is available on macOS only, and it requires the Computer Use feature to be enabled. It is also currently unavailable in the European Economic Area, the United Kingdom, and Switzerland. OpenAI frequently expands platform and regional support after an initial release, so Windows availability may follow — but for now, Mac is required.

What kinds of tasks work best with Record & Replay? ▼

Repetitive, multi-step tasks that follow the same path every time and require little human judgment. OpenAI's own examples include filing an expense, booking a parking space, creating a correctly configured issue, publishing a video, and downloading a recurring report. For small businesses, strong candidates include weekly report pulls, invoice and receipt filing, new-client onboarding setup, configured ticket creation, and content publishing checklists. Tasks that change constantly or need judgment at each step are a poor fit.

Is it safe to let ChatGPT watch and act on my screen? ▼

Record & Replay relies on Computer Use, which grants the AI permission to observe and act on your screen — so it's a meaningful trust decision that belongs on managed devices. The safest approach is to start with low-stakes tasks that retrieve or organize information (downloading reports, filing documents) rather than ones that move money, send external messages, or change production data. Review the generated SKILL.md so you understand exactly what each skill does, test with safe data first, and expand to higher-stakes workflows only once you trust the output.
