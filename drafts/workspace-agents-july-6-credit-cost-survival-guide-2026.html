Workspace Agents Stop Being Free July 6: The ChatGPT Business Credit-Cost Survival Guide

June 14, 2026
📖 10 min read
✍️ Sayfe.ai
News & Trends
10 min read

If your team has been running OpenAI's Workspace Agents for free, mark July 6, 2026 on the calendar. That's the day the extended free period ends and credit-based billing switches on for ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, Edu, and Teachers plans. OpenAI quietly pushed the original deadline back to give customers more runway — but the runway is now measured in a few short weeks, not months.

Here's the uncomfortable part: OpenAI has published a credit rate card, but it hasn't published a public dollar figure per credit for Business and Enterprise tiers. The rates are labeled "indicative" and roll out per organization through your admin console. Translation — the next three weeks are a budgeting exercise where you control every variable except the one your account team has to fill in. The teams that model their usage now will set sane spend caps before the meter starts. The teams that don't will get a surprise on the first invoice.

The 60-second version: Workspace Agents (and ChatGPT for Excel runs) move from free to credit-billed on July 6, 2026. Credits are consumed by token usage — input tokens, cached input tokens, and output tokens — not by a flat per-run fee. A typical end-to-end agent run on GPT-5.5 burns roughly 5 to 25 credits. Until OpenAI confirms your per-credit price, the smart move is to measure your real run volume now, set per-agent and per-day caps, and right-size which workflows actually deserve to run automatically.

What Actually Changes on July 6

Nothing about what Workspace Agents do is changing. They're still the shared, Codex-powered agents that live inside ChatGPT Business, plug into Slack and 60+ other apps, and run multi-step work in the background. (If you need the full primer on what they replaced, our deep dive on how Workspace Agents killed Custom GPTs covers it.)

What changes is the economics. During the research preview, every agent run was free. After July 6, every run draws down credits. The same shift applies to ChatGPT for Excel tasks, which also moved to the credit meter. Think of it like a company car that's been running on a full tank someone else paid for. The car didn't change. Starting July 6, you're buying the gas — and you'd want to know your mileage before you do.

Two facts make this manageable rather than scary. First, billing is consumption-based, so a workflow you rarely trigger costs almost nothing. Second, OpenAI's admin console lets you set hard credit caps per agent and per day. The risk isn't the pricing model — it's deploying agents without caps and discovering the bill after the fact.

How Credits Actually Get Counted

Forget per-run flat fees. A Workspace Agent run is priced on three token buckets, each with its own credit rate per 1 million tokens:

  • Input tokens — everything you and your connected apps feed the agent (the prompt, retrieved files, CRM records, email threads). The most expensive bucket.
  • Cached input tokens — repeated context the model has already seen, billed at a steep discount. This is why agents that reuse the same system instructions and reference files are cheaper than they look.
  • Output tokens — everything the agent generates (drafts, summaries, code, structured records).

OpenAI's worked example tells the real story: a run with 20,000 input tokens, 80,000 cached input tokens, and 5,000 output tokens comes to about 7.25 credits. Notice the shape of that — 80,000 of the 105,000 tokens were cached, and the run still landed in single digits. The lesson for budgeting is that context reuse is your biggest cost lever. An agent with a stable, cached knowledge base and tight outputs is dramatically cheaper than one that re-reads a fresh 100-page document on every trigger.

Workflow type Rough credits / run Cost driver to watch
Short single-tool task (classify a message, draft a reply) ~5 credits Output length
Standard multi-step run (the 7.25-credit example) 5–10 credits Fresh vs. cached input
Heavy run (web browsing + file generation + CRM writes) 15–25 credits Tool calls & new input tokens
Long-running background job (overnight reports, monitoring) 25+ credits, repeating Trigger frequency

Because the per-credit dollar price for Business isn't public yet, don't anchor on a dollar figure you saw in a forum. Anchor on credits per run × runs per day × working days. That gives you a credit budget you can hand your account team and a cap you can enforce today, regardless of where the final price lands.

⚠️ The single biggest mistake right now: wiring an agent into a high-volume Slack channel or shared inbox with no daily credit ceiling. A triage agent that fires on every inbound message can quietly 10x your run count the week a campaign goes out. Set per-agent and per-day caps in the admin console before July 6 — they default permissive.

Model Your Costs in 20 Minutes (The Worksheet)

You don't need a finance team for this. You need an honest count of what your agents actually do. Walk through these four numbers for each agent you're running:

  1. Runs per day. Open the admin console usage view and look at the last two weeks of preview activity. This is your real number, not your imagined one. Most teams over-estimate.
  2. Credits per run. Use the table above as a starting bracket, then refine with the per-run credit detail the console shows. A draft-a-reply agent is ~5; a research-and-write agent is ~15–25.
  3. Working days per month. ~22 for business-hours agents, ~30 for always-on monitors.
  4. Monthly credit budget = runs/day × credits/run × days. Set the per-day cap at roughly 1.5x your daily average so a busy day doesn't get throttled, but a runaway loop does.

A concrete example: a 10-person agency runs one lead-triage agent (40 runs/day at ~8 credits) and one weekly digest agent (1 run/week at ~25 credits). That's roughly 40 × 8 × 22 = 7,040 credits/month for triage, plus about 100 credits for the digest. Now you have a number to validate against your account team's per-credit quote — and a per-day cap (around 480 credits) that protects you the day an inbox blows up.

The 4 Moves to Make Before July 6

1. Inventory and rank your agents by value, not by count

Pull the list of active Workspace Agents from the admin console. For each one, ask a blunt question: if this run cost real money, would I still want it to fire? Kill the experiments nobody uses. The 80/20 rule is brutal here — usually two or three agents drive nearly all the value, and a long tail of half-built agents drive nothing but future credits.

2. Turn on caps everywhere, today

Set a per-agent credit cap and a workspace-wide daily ceiling now, while it's free, so the controls are tested and the team is used to them before billing flips on. A cap you configured calmly in June beats one you scramble to find after a surprise invoice in July.

3. Cache aggressively, output tightly

The 7.25-credit example proves the point: cached context is cheap, fresh input is not. Give each agent a stable instruction set and a fixed reference library so the model reuses cached tokens instead of re-ingesting everything each run. And constrain outputs — an agent told to "summarize in 5 bullet points" costs a fraction of one told to "write a full report" when a summary would do.

4. Get your per-credit price in writing

The one number you can't model is the one only OpenAI (or your channel partner) can give you. Before July 6, ask your account team for the per-credit price that will apply to your workspace and whether any initial credit grant comes with the feature. Walk in with your credit budget from the worksheet above — it turns a vague "how much will this cost?" into a precise "we'll run ~7,000 credits/month, what's our rate?"

Is It Still Worth It? (Honest ROI Math)

For most small teams, yes — but only for the agents that replace real recurring labor. The lead-triage agent above saves an account manager an hour or two a day. Even at a generous credit price, 7,000 credits a month against 30+ hours of recovered senior time is a trade most owners take without blinking. The math breaks the other way for "nice to have" agents that run constantly and save nobody any time. That's exactly why the inventory step matters more than the pricing step.

The competitive backdrop hasn't changed either. Microsoft and Google are still racing to match OpenAI's agent breadth, and as we covered in why ChatGPT Business is winning the SMB AI race, the connector ecosystem and model quality still favor OpenAI for the heterogeneous tool stacks most SMBs run. Credit billing doesn't erase that lead — it just means you now pay for the value instead of getting it free. The Honest Bottom Line

July 6 is not a crisis — it's a forcing function. The free preview let teams deploy agents without thinking about cost. Credit billing makes you think about it, which is healthy. The businesses that come out ahead will spend 30 minutes before the deadline doing three things: cutting the agents that don't earn their keep, setting hard caps on the ones that do, and getting a real per-credit price from their account team. Do that, and July 6 is a non-event. Ignore it, and the first invoice will do the prioritizing for you — at a price you didn't choose.

Frequently Asked Questions

When exactly does credit billing for Workspace Agents start? ▼

July 6, 2026. OpenAI extended the original free period to this date, after which Workspace Agent runs (and ChatGPT for Excel tasks) are billed on a credit basis for ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, Edu, and Teachers plans. Runs before that date remain free.

How much does one Workspace Agent run cost in credits? ▼

There's no flat per-run fee. Credits are consumed by token usage across three buckets — input, cached input, and output tokens. A typical end-to-end run on GPT-5.5 uses roughly 5 to 25 credits. OpenAI's published example of 20,000 input tokens, 80,000 cached input tokens, and 5,000 output tokens comes to about 7.25 credits. Short tasks land near the bottom of that range; heavy runs with browsing and file generation land near the top.

What is the dollar price per credit for ChatGPT Business? ▼

OpenAI has not published a public retail dollar figure per credit for the Business and Enterprise tiers — the rates shown are labeled "indicative" and are rolled out per organization through the admin console. To get the price that will apply to your workspace, ask your OpenAI account team or your channel partner directly. Walk in with a modeled credit budget so you can convert their quote into a real monthly number.

How do I keep Workspace Agent costs from running away? ▼

Set per-agent credit caps and a workspace-wide daily ceiling in the admin console before July 6. Inventory your agents and disable the ones nobody uses. Cache stable context and constrain output length so each run burns fewer tokens. Then watch usage closely for the first 30 days and adjust caps based on real data rather than estimates.

Are Workspace Agents available on ChatGPT Plus or Pro? ▼

No. Workspace Agents are available only on ChatGPT Business, Enterprise, Edu, and Teachers plans, and currently in research preview. ChatGPT Plus, Pro, and Free users do not have access to Workspace Agents. If your team is still managing AI through individual Plus seats, the agent capability is one of the strongest reasons to move to the Business plan.

Should small businesses still use Workspace Agents once they cost money? ▼

For agents that replace real recurring work — lead triage, weekly digests, invoice follow-up — yes. The recovered staff time almost always dwarfs the credit cost at small-team volumes. The trade breaks down only for "nice to have" agents that run constantly and save no one any time, which is exactly why inventorying and ranking your agents by value is the most important step before billing begins.
